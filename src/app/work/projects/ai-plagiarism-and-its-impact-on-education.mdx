## Overview

This research paper, conducted for English Language 5 at South East European University under Professor Hajrulla Hajrullai, investigates the impact of AI-generated content — particularly from chatbots like ChatGPT — on academic integrity and plagiarism in education. With 36 student participants from Pakistan, Tajikistan, and North Macedonia, the study uses quantitative survey methods to explore awareness, usage patterns, and moral perceptions surrounding AI-powered plagiarism.

## Key Findings

- **Plagiarism Awareness**: 94% of participants agreed or strongly agreed that plagiarism is the use of others' ideas and words without attribution, demonstrating strong baseline understanding of the concept.

- **AI-Generated Plagiarism Recognition**: 69.4% of participants agreed or strongly agreed that AI-generated content constitutes plagiarism, showing growing awareness of AI's role in academic dishonesty.

- **Widespread AI Usage**: 70% of surveyed students admitted to using AI chatbots like ChatGPT for various purposes, including homework and academic work, highlighting the prevalence of AI tools in education.

- **Mixed Moral Perceptions**: Opinions on the morality of using AI for academic tasks were divided — 58% agreed it was morally wrong, while 33% expressed a neutral stance, reflecting the ambiguity surrounding AI's ethical placement in academia.

## Literature Review Summary

The literature review covered several key perspectives:

- **Crawford, Cowling & Allen (2023)**: Noted nearly 5,000 published works on chatbots in higher education on Google Scholar, but only 8 peer-reviewed articles in Web of Science — highlighting a significant gap between public discourse and rigorous academic research on the topic.

- **Elali & Rachid (2023)**: Highlighted that AI-generated works can involve fabrication of data and false results, with AI technologies possessing distinct writing patterns detectable by AI detection tools. Warned about the erosion of credibility when inadequately authenticated AI systems are used for research.

- **Anders (2023)**: Argued against outright bans on AI, noting that AI is already embedded in tools like Microsoft Word and Grammarly. Proposed the concept of "AI literacy" encompassing: (1) awareness of AI's presence, (2) ability to harness its power, (3) knowledge that anyone can use it, and (4) critical thinking regarding AI content including methodology, sources, and biases.

- **Crawford et al. Conclusion**: Emphasized that AI should supplement learning, not substitute it, and recommended soft forms of encouraged support through chatbots for early assessment feedback and reading guidance.

## Methodology

The study employed a **quantitative research method** using anonymous surveys via Google Forms based on Likert-scale questionnaires.

**Participants**: 36 students aged 17–24 from Pakistan, Tajikistan, and North Macedonia.

**Survey Questions Covered**:

- Definition and understanding of plagiarism
- Classification of AI-generated content as plagiarism
- Personal usage of AI chatbots for homework, assignments, and exams
- Moral judgments on AI-assisted academic work
- Perceptions of peer AI usage in presentations and research
- Opinions on whether AI should be banned in education
- Long-term career impact of AI text generator misuse
- Comparison of chatbots vs. traditional search engines

## Challenges and Learnings

The research revealed a fundamental tension: while students broadly recognize AI-generated content as a form of plagiarism, a significant majority still use these tools for academic work. This gap between awareness and behavior suggests that policy alone won't solve the problem — education and guideline development are equally critical.

A key challenge in this research area is the lack of peer-reviewed literature relative to the speed of public discourse. With nearly 5,000 works on Google Scholar but only 8 peer-reviewed articles in Web of Science at the time of research, the field is moving faster than traditional academic publishing can track.

The research reinforced that AI is now an undeniable part of the educational landscape, and rather than pursuing prohibition-based approaches, institutions should focus on developing AI literacy among both students and educators.

## Recommendations

1. **Raise Awareness and Educate**: Workshops, seminars, and campaigns on the ethical implications of AI-generated plagiarism.

2. **Develop AI Guidelines**: Clear institutional policies on acceptable and responsible use of AI, including proper citation of AI-generated content.

3. **Integrate AI Ethics in Curricula**: Incorporate AI ethics and responsible technology use into relevant subjects.

4. **Implement Plagiarism Detection Software**: Invest in tools capable of identifying AI-generated content.

5. **Foster Academic Integrity Culture**: Promote honesty, originality, and proper citation through honor codes and integrity campaigns.

6. **Encourage Critical Thinking and Creativity**: Reward original work and independent thinking over AI-generated submissions.

7. **Collaboration Between AI Developers and Institutions**: Ensure responsible AI development with features that prioritize academic integrity.

8. **Continual Monitoring and Adaptation**: Proactive policy updates to keep pace with evolving AI technologies.

## Conclusion

This research confirms that AI chatbots like ChatGPT are actively used by students across multiple countries for academic tasks, from homework to presentations. While there is strong awareness that AI-generated content constitutes plagiarism, usage remains high, suggesting that awareness campaigns alone are insufficient.

The path forward requires a multi-faceted approach: education, guideline development, detection tools, and a cultural shift toward academic integrity that acknowledges AI as a permanent fixture in the educational landscape. Future research should explore the effectiveness of different integrity-promotion strategies and the potential role of AI itself in detecting and preventing AI-generated plagiarism.
